# 03 - Scale & Unit Economics

## Core question

Does charging restaurants for one-off apps create a scalable business, or just a service treadmill?

---

## 3.1 Revenue Model

Original claim: one-time setup fee per restaurant.

More realistic model:

- Setup fee: $500-$1,500.
- Monthly support: $50-$150.
- Optional marketing services: menu updates, social posts, Google profile management.

## 3.2 Unit Economics

| Metric | Value | Calculation |
|---|---:|---|
| Revenue / customer / year | $1,100-$3,300 | Setup plus support |
| COGS / customer / year | $150-$500 | Hosting, tools, payment setup, support |
| Gross margin | 70-85% before labor | Service time is the hidden cost |
| CAC | $100-$700 | Cold outreach, local ads, referrals |
| LTV | $1,000-$3,000 | Depends on retention and support plan |
| LTV/CAC | 2-5 | Works only with cheap local acquisition |
| Payback period | Immediate if setup fee closes |
| Monthly churn | Needs verification | Restaurant failure and low engagement risk |

## 3.3 Scale Ceiling

- One person can onboard perhaps 2-4 restaurants per month.
- Each active account creates support obligations.
- Beyond 20-40 active accounts, the founder needs help or a true multi-tenant product.

## 3.4 Recurring vs One-Time

One-time revenue is fragile. Every month starts at zero unless support revenue exists. App maintenance, menu updates, bug fixes, and payment issues all require recurring pricing.

## 3.5 Bull vs Bear

**Bull case**:

Productized service can produce cash quickly if the founder has a local sales channel and a tight template.

**Bear case**:

The business scales by adding customers and support work, not by software leverage. It can become a low-margin agency.

---

## Score: 5/10

**Reasoning**:

The economics can work as a local service, but the original one-time app model is not durable. It needs recurring support to avoid a constant sales treadmill.
