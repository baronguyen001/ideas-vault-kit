# 02 - Competition & Market

## Core question

Are restaurants underserved enough to pay a solo agency for custom iOS apps when many cheaper ordering tools already exist?

---

## 2.1 Market Validation

Restaurants do need online ordering, menu pages, loyalty, and direct customer relationships. Demand is real. The problem is that demand is already served by POS vendors, ordering widgets, website platforms, delivery apps, and local agencies.

### Market status

Selected: **saturated**

The market has many direct and adjacent options, including free or low-cost products. A new entrant needs a narrow local channel or a very specific workflow wedge.

---

### Adjacent substitutes

| Name | How restaurants use it instead |
|---|---|
| DoorDash / Uber Eats | Marketplace demand and online ordering |
| Google Business Profile | Discovery, menu, ordering links |
| Instagram / Facebook page | Menu, photos, messages, local updates |
| Local web agency | Website plus ordering without App Store risk |

### Big tech threat

Google, Apple, Meta, DoorDash, Uber, Square, and Toast all touch restaurant discovery or ordering. Distribution is the hard part, not app generation.

---

## 2.3 Niche Carve-Out

| Angle | Applies? | Specific niche |
|---|---|---|
| Vertical | Weak | One cuisine or restaurant type could work locally |
| Geography | Possible | A neighborhood or city-specific service |
| Persona | Possible | Non-technical owner-operators |
| Price wedge | Weak | Free/cheap tools already exist |
| Channel / UX | Stronger | PWA, QR menu, or messaging-first ordering |
| Bundle | Possible | Menu + website + Google profile + social updates |
| AI-first | Possible | Menu photos/copy/social posts, not native apps |

One-line differentiator if pivoted:

> Ordering pages for neighborhood restaurants that want direct orders without App Store overhead.

---

## 2.4 Pricing Power

- A one-time $3,000 app fee is hard to justify against low-cost tools.
- Realistic productized service: $500-$1,500 setup plus $50-$150/mo support.
- Recurring maintenance is mandatory because menus, devices, and platform requirements change.

## 2.5 Possible Moat

- [ ] Network effect
- [ ] Data moat
- [ ] Brand / community
- [x] Switching cost, if the restaurant uses it daily
- [ ] Regulatory moat
- [x] Speed / niche service
- [ ] Founder unfair advantage

## 2.6 Bull vs Bear

**Bull case**:

Some restaurants want done-for-you help and do not want to learn a generic SaaS. Local relationships can still win.

**Bear case**:

This is a crowded local service market with many cheaper substitutes and hard customer acquisition.

---

## Score: 4/10

**Reasoning**:

Demand is validated, but the market is saturated. A new entrant needs local distribution or a strong service bundle, not a generic "AI iOS app" pitch.
